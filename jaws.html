Jaws concentrates deep-ocean Pacific energy into towering waves, where long-period swells produce some of the heaviest surf on Earth.

Jaws breaks over a deep-water reef off Pe'ahi on Maui's north shore, where long-period North Pacific swells travel thousands of miles across open ocean before meeting a sudden rise in the seafloor. With no continental shelf to soften their approach, that distant energy arrives concentrated and intact, transforming open-water swell into some of the largest waves on Earth.

Unlike shallow, ledge-style reefs, Jaws draws its power from depth. The wave stands up gradually, loading mass and speed before breaking, producing towering open faces that move with relentless force. Control, commitment, and precise timing matter more than technical finesse, and even small miscalculations are amplified at scale.

Tow-in surfing wasn't about convenience; it was about access. It allowed surfers to enter waves at full momentum, matching the pace of a swell that had traveled thousands of miles across open ocean. At Jaws, that meant confronting faces taller than buildings, moving with relentless speed and power.

Hamilton's approach pushed the limits of what could be ridden. Waves once thought too large to surf became rideable, and Jaws emerged not just as a break, but as a benchmark for scale in big-wave surfing.
